### Context

ContextThis thesis is part of the ANR SOCLOUD project, which aims to explore the possibilities of designing a sustainable and open data center that respects planetary boundaries and is socially equitable toward its users. The goal is to go beyond mere energy efficiency concerns, whose gains are almost systematically offset by first-order or higher-order indirect negative effects, such as the rebound effect.

Practical Details and ApplicationThe thesis will be co-supervised between IRIT in Toulouse and FEMTO-ST in Besançon, with the recruited person working at the IRIT site in Toulouse. The SEPIA team at IRIT works on resource management in distributed systems and has extensive experience in energy-saving and sufficiency issues. The DEODIS team (FEMTO-ST) focuses on the design, optimization, and evaluation of distributed and intelligent systems. It is recognized for its work on energy-constrained scheduling and renewable energy management.

The recruited person will be supervised by Patricia Stolf, Professor at the University of Toulouse, Georges Da Costa, Professor at the University of Toulouse and Veronika Sonigo, associate professor at Université Marie et Louis Pasteur in Besançon. Funding will be provided through the SOCLOUD project, involving both academic and industrial partners. The PhD candidate will be integrated into a national collaborative environment with regular project meetings. The gross monthly salary will be approximately 2050 euros.

### Assignment

State of the Art and Problem PositioningLike many economic sectors, Information and Communication Technologies (ICT) contribute significantly to the pressure on planetary boundaries, a trend that is only intensifying. Data centers are particularly affected: their energy consumption, as well as their water and metal usage through hardware renewal, continues to grow. Numerous academic and industrial studies have focused on their energy efficiency, whether at the hardware level–by progressively improving individual components–or at the software level, for example, by optimizing resource allocation. However, indirect and systemic negative effects, such as the rebound effect [6], lead to an overall increase in energy consumption despite these efforts.

The ANR SOCLOUD project seeks to move beyond a purely technosolutionist approach by studying the conditions for implementing a sustainable data center. Sustainability is not limited to technical aspects; it also encompasses the dimension of usage, involving and empowering users in resource management issues [7]. One way to approach such a data center is to rely on the concept of &#34;convivial tool&#34; proposed by philosopher Ivan Illich [5] in the 1970s: such a tool respects planetary boundaries with understandable and easily explainable usage rules, is open and transparent in its decisions and trade-offs, equitable in its usage, and does not generate irreversible dependency.

Among the components of a data center’s software stack, resource managers (whether schedulers [8, 4] or orchestrators [3]) are the subject of intensive research, aiming to determine the best way to allocate CPU/GPU, RAM, or bandwidth according to one or more objective criteria. Scheduling problems, which constitute a particular class of resource allocation problems, have been studied since the 1960s [1]. In the context of data centers, most work focuses on efficiency and quality of service, which can take various forms (e.g., minimizing makespan, average flow time, or the number of tasks late relative to a deadline), but whose ultimate goal remains the same: allocating tasks or requests in a way that maximizes platform utilization while best meeting user needs. Energy efficiency is sometimes considered, but generally as a secondary criterion [2], and without ever questioning the usage patterns behind request execution.
